# Reflection — Music Recommender Simulation

## High-Energy Pop vs. Chill Lofi

These two profiles are almost mirror images of each other, and the results flipped in exactly the way the formula would predict. The High-Energy Pop user wants loud, happy, non-acoustic music, so songs like Sunrise City and Gym Hero rose to the top by scoring well on genre, mood, and high-energy proximity all at once. Switch to the Chill Lofi user — low energy, acoustic, calm mood — and the entire leaderboard inverts. Library Rain earned a perfect 5.50 because it hit every single scoring component: right genre, right mood, energy close enough, and acoustic. The Chill Lofi profile actually performed better than the pop profile in terms of score quality, which suggests the catalog has stronger lofi coverage than pop coverage. What changed between the two profiles was not the formula — it was which songs already existed in the catalog that happened to align perfectly with each set of preferences.

## Deep Intense Rock vs. the Metal/Chill Conflict

Both of these profiles involve heavier music, but the outcomes reveal something important about how much the genre weight controls results. The Deep Intense Rock profile worked cleanly: Storm Runner matched genre, mood, and energy, so it ranked first with a high score and the result felt right. The Metal/Chill conflict profile is where things got uncomfortable. Iron Cathedral ranked first even though the user wanted *chill* music and Iron Cathedral is not chill at all — but it is metal, and that +2.0 genre bonus was enough to outrun everything else. This is not a bug exactly, but it is a design consequence worth naming: when genre is worth twice as much as mood, you can get a "correct genre, wrong vibe" result and the formula will still call it the winner. If this were a real music app and someone asked for calm study metal but got aggressive thrash metal instead, they would be confused. The genre weight is doing a lot of heavy lifting, and sometimes that lifting is in the wrong direction.

## Rare Genre (Reggae) vs. Unknown Genre (K-Pop)

These two profiles both expose what happens when the catalog runs out of answers, but they fail in different ways. The reggae user got one genuinely great recommendation (Island Morning, 5.34) and then almost nothing — the score dropped to 2.46 for second place, and positions 2 through 5 were basically arbitrary. The catalog only has one reggae song, so once that song is placed first, the recommender has no meaningful signal left to rank the others. The k-pop user had a different problem: because no song in the catalog is tagged k-pop, the genre component never fired at all. The best possible score was 3.48, and even that felt hollow because the top result was not really a good match — it just happened to have the right energy and mood while the genre bonus stayed at zero for everything. The reggae case is like having one good answer and then silence. The k-pop case is like having no answers and pretending anyway. Both situations point to the same root cause: the catalog is too small and too narrow to serve users with anything outside its main genres.

## The Weight Experiment

Halving the genre weight and doubling the energy weight seemed like it might make energy-focused users happier, but the actual result was the opposite of useful. The top-ranked song stayed the same for all seven profiles — so in terms of "who wins," nothing changed. What changed was how *different* the scores felt from each other. Before the experiment, a song with no genre match might score around 2.5, which clearly signals "this is not a great fit." After the experiment, that same song could score 4.5 just by having close energy, which made it look nearly as good as a perfect match. The score range collapsed, and recommendations that should have felt clearly worse started blending in with the good ones. This taught a useful lesson: the purpose of weights is not just to push the right song to the top — it is also to create meaningful distance between good matches and mediocre ones. When energy dominated, the system lost its ability to say "this is clearly better than that."

## A Note on Gym Hero

Gym Hero keeps appearing near the top of high-energy lists even when the user is not specifically asking for pop music. To understand why, imagine the scoring formula as a checklist: the more boxes a song checks, the higher it scores. Gym Hero is an extremely high-energy song (0.93 out of 1.0), which means it scores close to the maximum on energy proximity for anyone targeting high energy. Even if it misses on genre, energy proximity alone contributes up to 2.0 points — and Gym Hero almost always collects that full amount. In plain terms: Gym Hero is just a really intense song, and for users who want high energy, that single attribute is valuable enough to keep it in the top results almost regardless of what genre they requested. It is a reminder that in a small catalog, one song that scores very high on a heavily weighted feature will keep appearing whether you want it to or not.
